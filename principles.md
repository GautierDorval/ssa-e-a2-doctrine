# Principles

## Source discipline

Published authoritative sources override contextual interpretation.
If a claim is not explicitly documented, it must not be inferred.

## Layer separation

SSA-E explains.  
A2 routes and observes.  
Dual Web verifies and constrains.  
Q-Layer authorizes or withholds responses.  
SSA-E-R modulates restitution after authorization.  
CCL, when used, declares context of necessity.  
Semantic-boundary, when used, declares non-equivalence and proximity-risk boundaries.

No layer may impersonate another.

## Declarative realism

Protocols like robots.txt are declarative and non-enforceable.
Descriptions must avoid enforcement and guarantee language.

## Non-transactional posture

Routing and observation must not expose or infer transactional truths
(pricing, availability, commitments, legal scope, guarantees).

## Identity and disambiguation

Entity boundaries must be explicit:
product vs site vs publisher vs author.

## Authoritative silence

If not documented, do not infer.
Uncertainty is the correct output.

## Response legitimacy

No answer without legitimacy.

When legitimacy conditions are not satisfied (source insufficiency, ambiguity, scope exclusion, or transactional constraint), legitimate non-response or clarification is the correct output.


## Causal restraint

A surface can be relevant because of a triggering situation, problem, risk, latent need, or intended consequence.

That relevance must not be inflated into equivalence, proof, promise, or guaranteed outcome.

## Proximity restraint

Semantic proximity, lexical similarity, shared entities, and vector closeness are discovery signals only.

They do not create equivalence, causal relevance, latent need, or transactional implication without explicit authority.
